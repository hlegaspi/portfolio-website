ChitChat: Language App

ChitChat is a language app where users can practice using a language through text messaging. The duration of the project lasted 1 month and my roles included UI/UX designer, UX researcher, and creating a business proposal. The objective of the project was to design the iOS and Android version of a mobile app of your choice that solves a specific user problem.

My Process

Research (Competitive Analysis)

Duolingo is a very well-known, well-established, and popular app due to its innovative UX and UI design. However, it’s focus is on memorization (e.g. games, quizzes) and its onboarding process is lengthy.

MemRise’s UI Design has great colors and allows the user to know where they are in the app. However the overall visual hierarchy is a bit cluttered. Also, their focus is on memorization through games and quizzes like Duolingo. Although, their onboarding process is much simpler.

Priority Matrix

This tool was used to determine the mobile app’s main functions. The two tasks that were given the highest priority were the AI Messaging and Translator. Memorization games have already been used frequently by other apps. Then MemRise already had travel language courses. While AI Messaging was much more difficult to produce, it was unique and would set the app apart. A translator would also be necessary for the user learning a new language.

Userflow

In the onboarding process, users will see welcome screens with information on the app (however, they can easily skip this). They can then choose their native language and foreign language and then sign up. From the home screen, users can either text or translate.

Wireframes

The iOS and Android wireframes differ in various UI elements. One example is the buttons. The Android buttons and dropdowns are more 3-dimensional and elevated than the 2-dimensional iOS. Also, the Android design utilizes radio buttons. However, each design for the two devices maintain the key functions of the app.

Style Guide

The mobile app’s visual design is modern but playful. It utilizes simple geometric shapes in its design, especially its illustrations. The colors are bright to draw the user’s attention.

Mockups

Users are welcomed with information about the app but have the option to skip it. This was added as some feedback showed that the app’s concept was not clear. From the home screen, users can visit the message screen to chat with “ChatBot” or translate words using the translator screen. A navigation bar is provided for users to jump to various locations. The android version of the mobile app utilizes material design guidelines and varies from the iOS in some UI components.

Conclusion

Overall, I’ve learned a lot throughout this process. First, making mistakes are going to happen. What’s more important is recognizing them and learning from them. Second, always conduct feedback throughout the process. This will help keep the product user-centered and improve it vastly. If I could go back and improve on something, it would be to conduct more thorough UX research. I learned a little later in the process that Duolingo had a similar feature in the past but was discontinued because not many people were using it. I’d like to explore this idea more and look for ways to improve it.
